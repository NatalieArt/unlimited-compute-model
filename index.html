Why Unlimited AI Needs a Different Compute Model

By Mauvis Ledford, CEO·July 2026

Every few months, an AI company launches an unlimited generation plan. Creators celebrate. A few months later, the plan quietly disappears — closed to new subscribers, converted to credits, "evolved" into something metered. This has now happened enough times that it's not an anecdote; it's a pattern:

December 2024

Pika discontinues its Unlimited plan

moving users to a credit-based tier. The stated reason: "unsustainable due to misuse." source

June 2025

Hailuo (MiniMax) closes its $94.99 Unlimited plan to new buyers

"unlimited" now covers only the older Hailuo-01 model, with flagship models gated behind credits on $124.99–$199.99 tiers. source

March 2026

OpenAI announces the shutdown of its Sora app

after Forbes reported the free video feed was costing up to an estimated $15 million per day in compute. source

April 2026

Freepik

which launched unlimited AI generation to real acclaim in July 2025, reintroduces credits for premium models — roughly ten models stay free as it rebrands to Magnific. Unlimited lasted nine months. source

June 1, 2026

Runway closes its $95/month Unlimited plan to new subscribers

existing customers migrate to metered credits ("Max") on September 1. Two details worth knowing: the Unlimited plan reportedly carried the same 2,250 monthly credits as the $35 Pro plan for fast generations, and the migration removes Explore Mode — the throttled free-generation queue that made "unlimited" true at all. source

And it's not just creative tools. On June 1, 2026, GitHub Copilot moved every plan to usage-based billing (source). OpenAI's Nick Turley has publicly compared unlimited AI plans to selling "unlimited electricity." The industry consensus is congealing: the AI subsidy era is ending, flat-rate is dead, everything becomes a meter.

We just launched an unlimited plan. Here's why we believe the consensus diagnosed the right disease and prescribed the wrong cure.

The problem was never "unlimited." It was the cost structure underneath it.

When a centralized AI company sells you unlimited generation, it is arbitraging a fixed, brutal cost: enterprise GPUs rented from clouds, or bought outright. An NVIDIA H100 runs roughly $30,000; the cloud rental meter never stops. Every marginal render is real marginal cost against that bill, so a flat-rate plan is a bet that users won't use it — and creative tools are iteration machines. The heaviest users are the most passionate customers. The plan works right up until the product succeeds, and then the GPU bill eats it. That's the story of every retreat above.

Sogni's cost structure is inverted. We render on the Sogni Supernet — a network of independent GPU operators, most running consumer cards like the RTX 4090 and 5090. We don't rent their hardware at a fixed rate. Instead, 51% of net subscription revenue (net of payment fees, taxes, and refunds) accrues to a monthly worker pool, split by each operator's share of completed rendering work. Our compute cost is a percentage of revenue by construction. There is no fixed cloud bill to underrun. When subscribers grow, the pool grows, which recruits more supply — the same demand-attracts-supply dynamic that lets a rideshare network absorb demand spikes without owning cars.

Consumer GPUs are the right tool for this specific job

The obvious objection: consumer cards can't compete with data-center hardware. For some workloads that's true. For this one, the economics run the other way:

  • Price per unit of hardware: an RTX 5090 lists around $2,000 versus roughly $30,000 for an H100 — 15× — while a 2025 peer-reviewed ACM study measured consumer RTX 4090 clusters delivering 62–78% of H100 throughput for LLM inference at up to 75% lower cost per token. (source)
  • The workload fits the hardware: the most popular open image and video models — the ones the ComfyUI community actually runs — are distilled, turbo, fp8-optimized variants engineered to fit 24–32GB of consumer VRAM. Serving them on consumer silicon isn't a compromise; it's the native habitat. Slower per job in some cases, decisively cheaper per output.
  • Idle supply: operators run hardware they already own, earning in hours it would otherwise sit dark. The Supernet holds substantial spare render capacity today — unlimited plans are, in effect, selling compute that currently goes unused.

The open-model wave makes the timing

The second thing the retreats have in common: they were built on closed, per-render-licensed or self-trained frontier models — the most expensive possible substrate for an unlimited promise. Meanwhile, open-weight models crossed a quality threshold that most of the market hasn't priced in. Lightricks' LTX-2.3 — open weights, synchronized audio+video, up to 4K — was downloaded more than 2.1 million times on Hugging Face last month (source). Alibaba's Z-Image Turbo: nearly one million (source). Krea 2 went open-weights on June 22 and was live on Sogni within days (source).

The price gap is not subtle. On fal.ai — a popular GPU marketplace — a 15-second 1080p clip from ByteDance's frontier Seedance 2.0 model runs about $10.23 at posted rates ($0.682/second), versus under $1 for the open LTX-2.3 Fast model at the same length and resolution (source 1, source 2). More than ten-to-one, same marketplace, same clip. Frontier models are extraordinary, and we serve them — pay-as-you-go, priced honestly, because per-render vendor licensing can never hide inside a flat fee. But the creative volume — the iteration, the drafts, the 40 versions before the keeper — belongs on open models, where the marginal cost supports it.

That's the design of Sogni Unlimited: $20/month, fair-use unlimited across the open models we host — image, video, music, and LLM chat — with the three frontier vendor models (GPT Image 2, Seedance 2.0, HappyHorse 1.1) as metered add-ons at a member discount.

"Unlimited with fair use" — what we mean, precisely

Skepticism is warranted; the word has been abused. So here's the mechanics, published rather than buried: covered renders spend no credits — there is no monthly meter, nothing you buy and burn down. Fair use governs scheduling: each tier has published concurrency limits, exceptionally heavy sustained use queues behind other jobs at peak, and heavy-usage throttles reset each UTC day. Queued work gets done. The difference between this and "unlimited" plans that quietly suspend their heaviest users is that we're telling you the mechanics up front — they're the reason the promise is sustainable.

Can demand outrun the network? Three answers, in order: today there is substantial headroom; the 51% revenue share recruits supply as demand grows (and we can provision additional support nodes through rental partners like Salad profitably, because pool economics cover it); and the priority ladder protects everyone — paid pay-as-you-go jobs first, then Unlimited by tier, then free-tier jobs.

The bet

Every company that retreated from unlimited was making the same wager against its own users: please don't use this too much. We're making the opposite wager: that if compute costs scale with revenue instead of against it, heavy use is the business model, not the threat to it. One year and 158 million renders into the Supernet's public life, that's a bet we're structurally built to make — and the people who own the GPUs get paid 51% of net revenue to help us win it.

Iteration is where creative work actually happens. It shouldn't have a taxi meter running.
